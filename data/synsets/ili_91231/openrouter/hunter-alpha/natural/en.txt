Marrano is a derogatory historical term used in medieval Spain and Portugal to refer to Jews who converted to Christianity under pressure but continued to observe Jewish rites in secret. The word carries strong pejorative connotations and was employed by Old Christians to denigrate these converts, whom they suspected of insincere faith. During the Spanish Inquisition, Marranos faced systematic persecution, interrogation, and punishment by ecclesiastical tribunals tasked with rooting out heresy. The term is closely related to "Converso," though Converso is a more neutral designation for any Jewish or Moorish convert to Christianity, while Marrano specifically implies the accusation of hidden Judaizing. Today, historians use the term cautiously due to its offensive etymological origins, often preferring "crypto-Jew" or "anusim" in scholarly discourse.
