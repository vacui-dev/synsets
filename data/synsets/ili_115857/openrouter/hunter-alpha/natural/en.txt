Pepsinogen is a zymogen — a biologically inactive enzyme precursor — produced by the chief cells lining the stomach walls. When exposed to hydrochloric acid in the gastric environment, it undergoes a conformational change and is cleaved into pepsin, the primary proteolytic enzyme responsible for breaking down dietary proteins into smaller peptides. As a member of the broader family of enzymes, pepsinogen exemplifies the zymogen activation mechanism common in digestive biochemistry. This inactive form serves a protective function, preventing the enzyme from digesting the stomach's own tissues before activation. The conversion of pepsinogen to pepsin represents a critical early step in the protein digestion process.
