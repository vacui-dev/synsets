Ctenophora, commonly known as comb jellies, is a phylum of exclusively marine invertebrate animals. They are characterized by biradial symmetry and eight rows of ciliary combs used for locomotion, which give the group its name. Despite superficial resemblance to jellyfish, ctenophores lack stinging cells and belong to a separate evolutionary lineage.

The phylum comprises approximately 150 described species divided into two classes: Tentaculata, which bear retractile tentacles for prey capture, and Nuda, which lack tentacles entirely. Ctenophores are voracious predators of zooplankton and fish larvae, playing a significant ecological role in marine food webs.

Most species are hermaphroditic and reproduce sexually, though some can regenerate extensively from body fragments. Formerly classified within Coelenterata alongside cnidarians, Ctenophora was elevated to independent phylum status following molecular phylogenetic analyses. However, its precise position relative to other basal animal lineages remains an active area of research.
