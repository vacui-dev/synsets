A vibrio is any bacterium belonging to the genus Vibrio, characterized by its distinctive comma-shaped or curved rod morphology and motility via polar flagella. These gram-negative microorganisms are widely distributed in aquatic environments, particularly in marine and estuarine waters, where they constitute a significant component of the natural microbiota. Several species are notable human pathogens, most notably Vibrio cholerae, which causes cholera—a severe diarrheal disease transmitted through contaminated water and food. Other clinically important species include Vibrio vulnificus, associated with wound infections from seawater exposure, and Vibrio parahaemolyticus, a common cause of seafood-borne gastroenteritis. The genus belongs to the family Vibrionaceae within the class Gammaproteobacteria, and its members are facultatively anaerobic, oxidase-positive organisms that thrive in warm, nutrient-rich waters.
