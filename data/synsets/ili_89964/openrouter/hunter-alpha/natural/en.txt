A fire watcher was a civilian volunteer during the Second World War in Britain whose primary duty was to patrol designated areas and spot incendiary bombs dropped by enemy aircraft during air raids. Operating as part of the broader civil defense network, these individuals stood watch on rooftops, in streets, and across neighborhoods throughout the Blitz, ready to raise the alarm and tackle small fires before they could spread into devastating conflagrations. The role was distinct from that of a military guard or professional warden — fire watchers were ordinary citizens who took shifts through the night, equipped with little more than buckets of sand, stirrup pumps, and a keen sense of vigilance. Their contribution was vital to urban survival during the sustained bombing campaigns of 1940–1941, when entire districts of London, Coventry, and other cities were set ablaze by waves of incendiary ordnance.
