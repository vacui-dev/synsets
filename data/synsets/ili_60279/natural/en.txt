A time-fuse is an ignition device engineered to burn at a predictable rate over a predetermined duration, serving primarily in military ordnance and demolition to trigger explosions after a controlled delay. The device consists of a cord or tube filled with combustible material that, once ignited, burns steadily toward a detonator or main explosive charge. Unlike instantaneous fuses that detonate immediately upon activation, time-fuses allow the operator to establish a safe interval between ignition and the resulting blast. Time-fuses have been used extensively in artillery shells, hand grenades, and mining operations where precise timing of the detonation is critical. The burning rate depends on the composition and thickness of the fuse material, and standardized time-fuses are calibrated to combust at known rates measured in seconds per unit length.
