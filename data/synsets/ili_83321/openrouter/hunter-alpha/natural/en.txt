The Arabian Peninsula is a major peninsula in Southwest Asia, bounded by the Red Sea to the west and the Persian Gulf to the east. It serves as the heartland of the Arab world and has been a crossroads of ancient trade routes, including the historic incense and spice trade networks that connected Mediterranean civilizations with the East.

The peninsula's economy was historically driven by nomadic pastoralism and trade, but the discovery of vast petroleum reserves in the twentieth century fundamentally transformed its political and economic landscape. Today, the region is the world's largest exporter of crude oil, with Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, and Oman among its wealthiest nations.

Geographically, the interior is dominated by the vast Rub' al Khali desert — one of the largest sand deserts on Earth — with an arid climate supporting limited agriculture along coastal strips. The Islamic faith, which originated in the Hejaz region of present-day Saudi Arabia, remains central to the peninsula's cultural and political identity, shaping governance, law, and daily life across its constituent states.
