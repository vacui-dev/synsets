Osage is a critically endangered Dhegiha dialect of the Siouan language family, historically spoken by the Osage Nation across the prairies of present-day Missouri, Arkansas, and Oklahoma. As one of five closely related Dhegiha languages — alongside Omaha-Ponca, Kansa, Quapaw, and Kaw — Osage shares deep structural and lexical similarities with its sister dialects while maintaining distinctive phonological features that reflect the unique cultural identity of the Osage people. The language served as a primary vehicle of oral tradition, ceremonial practice, and social governance for centuries before European contact, encoding intricate knowledge systems related to kinship, ecology, and spirituality. Today, revitalization efforts are underway following decades of suppression under assimilationist boarding school policies, with community-led programs working to document and transmit Osage to new generations of speakers.
