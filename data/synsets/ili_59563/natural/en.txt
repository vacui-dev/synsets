A stereo, also known as a stereoscopic photograph or stereoscopic picture, is a pair of photographs taken from slightly different angles that produce a three-dimensional effect when viewed together. This imaging technique exploits the same principle as human binocular vision — each eye perceives a scene from a slightly different position, and the brain combines these two perspectives to reconstruct depth. By mimicking this natural parallax, a stereo creates the illusion of spatial depth on an otherwise flat surface, allowing the viewer to perceive the photographed scene as having volume and distance rather than appearing as a conventional two-dimensional image.

The stereoscopic photograph was popularized in the mid-19th century, when handheld stereoscopes became a common form of home entertainment. Specialized dual-lens cameras captured paired exposures on a single photographic plate or across two separate negatives. When placed in a stereoscope viewer, the left image would be presented only to the left eye and the right image only to the right eye, producing a vivid sense of depth that astonished Victorian audiences.

In modern usage, the term has expanded beyond classical photography to encompass digital stereoscopic images used in virtual reality, 3D cinema, and scientific visualization. Whether rendered on film or on a digital display, the underlying mechanism remains the same: two offset perspectives are delivered separately to each eye, and the visual system reconstructs a three-dimensional percept from the disparity between them. Anaglyph images, which use contrasting colors to separate the two views, represent one well-known format for presenting stereoscopic content without specialized hardware.
