In anatomy, a tube-shaped structure is any hollow, cylindrical body structure that serves as a conduit or passage for fluids, gases, or other materials within an organism. These structures are fundamental components of biological systems, forming channels such as blood vessels, ducts, and respiratory passages. The walls of a tube-shaped structure are typically composed of specialized tissue layers — often including an inner epithelial lining — that regulate the transport of substances through the lumen, the central cavity. Found throughout the body in organs like the fallopian tubes, Eustachian tubes, and bronchi, tube-shaped structures enable essential functions such as circulation, secretion, and respiration. As anatomical structures, they are classified under the broader category of body structures studied in anatomy, distinguished from solid organs by their enclosed, empty interior.
