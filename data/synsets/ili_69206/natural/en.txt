Federalism is a political ideology centered on the principle of distributing governmental authority between a central governing body and constituent political units, such as states or provinces. Rooted in the broader tradition of political theory, federalism advocates for a system in which these self-governing units retain meaningful autonomy while participating in a larger unified structure. Unlike unitary systems that concentrate power at the national level, federal arrangements allow regional governments to legislate on matters such as education, taxation, and criminal law within their jurisdictions. The concept has shaped the constitutional frameworks of numerous modern democracies, including the United States, Germany, Canada, and Australia, each adapting federal principles to suit their unique historical and cultural contexts. Federalism thus represents a pragmatic middle ground between the extremes of centralized absolutism and fragmented separatism, seeking to balance national cohesion with local self-determination.
