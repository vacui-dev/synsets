Antivenin (also spelled antivenene) is a biological preparation composed of antibodies that specifically neutralizes the effects of venom introduced into the body through the bite or sting of venomous animals, including snakes, spiders, scorpions, and certain insects. It is classified as a specialized type of antitoxin, which itself belongs to the broader category of antibodies — proteins produced by the immune system to identify and counteract foreign substances. Antivenins are typically produced by immunizing donor animals, such as horses or sheep, with small doses of a particular venom, then harvesting and purifying the resulting antibodies from their blood serum. The administration of antivenin is often a critical emergency medical intervention, particularly in regions where encounters with venomous snakes or arachnids are common, as untreated envenomation can lead to tissue damage, paralysis, or death. Different antivenins are specific to particular species or groups of venomous animals, meaning that accurate identification of the offending creature is essential for effective treatment.
