A tenterhook is one of a series of evenly spaced metal hooks mounted along the edges of a tenter, a wooden or metal frame used in the textile industry to stretch freshly woven or dyed cloth while it dries. The hooks grip the selvage or edges of the fabric, keeping it taut and preventing shrinkage, wrinkling, or distortion as moisture evaporates. Tenterhooks were essential in the finishing stages of wool and cotton production, particularly during the 18th and 19th centuries when the English textile mills relied on tenter fields — open areas where lengths of cloth were pinned to large frames and left to dry in the open air. The word is best known today through the idiom "to be on tenterhooks," meaning to be in a state of anxious suspense or nervous anticipation, a metaphor drawn from the tension the hooks impose on the stretched fabric.
