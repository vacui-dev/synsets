Carl David Anderson (1905–1991) was an American experimental physicist best known for his 1932 discovery of the positron, the antiparticle of the electron. Working at the California Institute of Technology, he observed the positron while studying cosmic rays using a cloud chamber, a finding that provided the first experimental evidence for antimatter. Anderson shared the 1936 Nobel Prize in Physics with Victor Hess for this groundbreaking work. His discovery confirmed Paul Dirac's theoretical prediction of antimatter and opened a new chapter in particle physics research.
