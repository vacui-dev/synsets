Serrulate is a botanical term describing a leaf margin that bears very fine, minute teeth typically visible only under magnification. Derived from the Latin "serrula" meaning small saw, this adjective is widely used in plant taxonomy and morphological descriptions to characterize subtle edge patterns on foliage. The term represents the finest grade of serration, distinguishing it from coarser tooth patterns such as serrate or crenate margins that are visible to the naked eye. Serrulate edges are commonly found in species belonging to families such as Rosaceae and Betulaceae, where the delicate sawtooth morphology may serve protective functions against herbivory or assist in channeling water droplets away from the leaf surface. Botanists rely on this precise terminology when documenting plant characteristics in taxonomic keys, field guides, and herbarium specimen descriptions.
