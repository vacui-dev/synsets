A person having kinship with another or others is an individual bound to someone else through ties of blood, marriage, or adoption. This concept captures the fundamental social identity of being a member of a family unit, whether that connection arises from shared ancestry, marital union, or legal arrangement. Such a person may be a blood relative — descended from a common ancestor — or connected through marriage as an in-law, or formally brought into the family through adoption. The term encompasses the full spectrum of familial relations, from immediate relatives like parents, children, and siblings to more distant connections such as cousins and second cousins. In anthropological and everyday usage, this person constitutes the basic building block of kinship systems that organize human societies across cultures.
