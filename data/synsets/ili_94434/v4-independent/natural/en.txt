Pope Benedict XV, born Giacomo della Chiesa (1854–1922), was the 258th pope of the Roman Catholic Church, serving from 1914 until his death in 1922. Elected just weeks after the outbreak of World War I, his pontificate was largely defined by the catastrophic conflict that engulfed Europe and beyond. Benedict XV is best remembered for his tireless humanitarian efforts during the war, particularly his establishment of the Vatican Information Bureau in 1915, a service dedicated to locating prisoners of war and facilitating communication between them and their families across enemy lines. He issued the papal peace note of 1917, known as "Dès le début," which proposed a seven-point plan for ending the conflict through negotiation rather than total victory, though both sides largely rejected his appeals. Beyond wartime diplomacy, Benedict XV reformed papal finances, promoted missionary work in non-Christian lands, and initiated the codification of canon law. His relatively short but consequential papacy is often regarded as a bridge between the more conservative era of Pius X and the social engagement that characterized later twentieth-century popes.
