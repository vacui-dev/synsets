Thraco-Phrygian is a proposed and now extinct branch of the Indo-European language family that encompasses the ancient languages spoken by the Thracian and Phrygian peoples. This linguistic grouping remains hypothetical and controversial among historical linguists, with some scholars suggesting a closer relationship to Armenian based on shared linguistic features and geographical proximity in the ancient Balkans and Anatolia. The Thraco-Phrygian branch consists primarily of two attested languages: Thracian, spoken in the eastern Balkans including parts of modern Bulgaria, Greece, and Turkey; and Phrygian, spoken in central and western Anatolia in what is now Turkey. Both languages are poorly attested, surviving mainly through fragmentary inscriptions, glosses recorded by ancient Greek and Roman authors, and proper names. Thracian disappeared by the early Middle Ages, absorbed by Greek, Latin, and eventually Slavic languages, while Phrygian survived slightly longer with inscriptions dating into the Roman period. The proposed connection to Armenian suggests that all three may have formed a distinct sub-group within Indo-European, though this relationship remains debated due to the limited corpus of surviving texts and the challenges of linguistic reconstruction from such sparse evidence.
