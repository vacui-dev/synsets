Takelma is an extinct Penutian language that was historically spoken by the Takelma people in the interior valleys of southwestern Oregon. It belongs to the broader family of Penutian languages found across the Pacific Northwest and central California, a group characterized by certain shared structural features among the indigenous languages of the region. The Takelma people lived along the Rogue River and its tributaries, and their language reflected the ecological and cultural landscape of that territory. Linguist Edward Sapir documented Takelma in the early twentieth century, producing one of the most detailed grammatical descriptions of any Native American language of Oregon. Today, no fluent speakers of Takelma remain, making it a critically endangered language, though revitalization efforts by descendants of the Takelma people seek to preserve what is known of the language's vocabulary and structure.
